**Mistake #5: No Strategy for Shoulder Seasons**

Every property has peak seasons when demand is high and shoulder seasons when occupancy drops. Most owners accept lower occupancy during shoulder periods as inevitable. This is a mistake. Shoulder seasons are opportunities for strategic revenue generation through packages, partnerships, and targeted marketing.

Partner with local businesses to create experience packages. A wine country inn might partner with vineyards for exclusive tastings. A mountain lodge could offer guided hiking with local outfitters. After fifteen years consulting with boutique hotels, bed-and-breakfasts, and luxury vacation rentals, I've identified the single most expensive mistake property owners make: treating pricing as a cost-recovery exercise rather than a demand-optimization strategy.

Here's what I mean. Most property owners price rooms based on covering costs plus desired profit margin. A room costs one hundred dollars to service (cleaning, utilities, overhead), so they charge two hundred dollars to achieve fifty percent margin. This pricing stays static regardless of demand signals.

This approach ignores the fundamental principle of revenue management: your inventory is perishable. An unsold room tonight generates zero revenue forever. You can't stockpile unused rooms for high-demand periods. This creates a unique optimization challenge that cost-plus pricing completely fails to address.

**The Alternative: Demand-Based Dynamic Pricing**

I worked with a forty-room boutique hotel in Charleston that was charging one hundred seventy-five dollars per night year-round. Their occupancy averaged sixty-two percent. They were leaving massive revenue on the table during high-demand periods and failing to drive occupancy during low-demand periods.

We implemented dynamic pricing based on real-time demand signals: local events, competitor rates, booking pace, and historical patterns. During high-demand weekends (Charleston Wine & Food Festival, Spoleto Festival), rates increased to three hundred fifty dollars. During low-demand weekdays, we created value-add packages at one hundred ninety-five dollars that felt premium, not discounted.

Result: one hundred forty-four percent revenue increase in six months. Same property. Same rooms. Strategic pricing.

**The Psychology of Pricing**

What made this work wasn't just the math‚Äîit was understanding guest psychology. Guests don't want discounts. They want value. A room discounted from two hundred dollars to one hundred fifty dollars feels cheap. A room at one hundred ninety-five dollars that includes breakfast, wine tasting, and late checkout feels premium.

We stopped discounting and started packaging. We stopped reacting to competition and started leading with value. We stopped treating all rooms equally and started segmenting inventory by desirability.

**The Bigger Lesson**

This applies beyond hospitality. Whether you're pricing hotel rooms, consulting services, or SaaS products, the principle is the same: price based on value delivered and demand signals, not cost recovery.
